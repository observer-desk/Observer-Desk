THE RICE BETRAYAL

How a state monopoly, compromised land reform, and decades of missed opportunities left the Philippines dependent on imported rice.

⏱ 15 min read
Agriculture • Rice • Governance • Philippines
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
Why is the Philippines still struggling with rice after nearly fifty years of reforms, studies, and government programs? Every administration promised solutions. Every administration had economists, agriculture experts, and technocrats. Yet by 2026, the Philippines had become the world's largest rice importer. This investigation examines the policy decisions that transformed a rice-growing nation into the world's largest buyer of imported rice.

The 2018 Warning Shot

In August 2018, National Food Authority rice disappeared from many Metro Manila markets. Commercial rice prices reached ₱60-70 per kilo in some areas, while inflation climbed to 6.7%, the highest in nine years. The immediate response was familiar: politicians blamed traders, traders blamed government, and government blamed supply shortages. Six months later, Congress passed the Rice Tariffication Law.

But 2018 was not the beginning of the crisis. It was the moment decades of policy failures became impossible to ignore.

The more important question is this: Why is the Philippines still struggling with rice after nearly fifty years of reforms, studies, and government programs? Every administration had economists, agriculture experts, and technocrats. Every administration produced plans and promised solutions. Yet by 2026, the Philippines had become the world's largest rice importer.

The problem was never a lack of knowledge. The country knew it needed irrigation, affordable credit, mechanization, and modern farm infrastructure. Those solutions have appeared in policy papers for decades. What was missing was the political will to pursue them consistently.

The rice crisis of 2018 was not a surprise. It was the bill finally coming due for decisions made between 1972 and 2019.

Central Question: Why did a country with fertile land, a tropical climate, and generations of rice farmers become the world's largest rice importer?

I. 1972: NATIONAL FOOD AUTHORITY MONOPOLY

Policy: Presidential Decree No. 4. Only the National Food Authority could legally import rice. Private traders were prohibited from importing until 2019.

At the time, the logic appeared sound. Rice was not just another commodity. It was the staple food of most Filipino families. Policymakers believed that allowing government to control rice imports would protect consumers from shortages, stabilize prices, and shield farmers from volatile global markets.

For nearly five decades, the NFA sat at the center of Philippine rice policy. If rice needed to be imported, the NFA would import it. If buffer stocks needed to be maintained, the NFA would maintain them. If prices rose too quickly, the NFA was expected to intervene.

The problem was that the agency was attempting to satisfy two groups with opposing interests. Farmers wanted higher prices for their harvests. Consumers wanted lower prices at the market. Government promised both.

That created a structural problem. To support farmers, the NFA had to buy rice at favorable prices. To support consumers, it often sold rice below cost. The difference was absorbed through borrowing and government support.

Over time, these losses accumulated.

Commission on Audit reports show that NFA net losses exceeded ₱100 billion between 2005 and 2018. Debt reached approximately ₱177 billion in 2010 and climbed to around ₱187 billion by 2016.

Supporters argued that these losses were the unavoidable cost of food security. Critics argued that the country was spending billions on market intervention without adequately investing in the long-term productivity improvements that could reduce production costs.

NFA's Core Contradiction: Farmers wanted higher prices. Consumers wanted lower prices. Government promised both—and taxpayers financed the gap.

The weaknesses of the system became most visible during the 2008 global rice crisis.

As rice-exporting countries restricted exports and prices surged worldwide, the Philippines scrambled to secure supplies. NFA purchased rice at prices exceeding $1,100 per metric ton, compared to normal levels of roughly $400 per metric ton. Commission on Audit reports later showed that NFA losses reached approximately ₱40 billion in 2008 alone.

The crisis exposed a major vulnerability. The country was relying on government intervention to compensate for weaknesses in domestic production rather than addressing those weaknesses directly.

Between 2010 and 2016, the government infused more than ₱120 billion to help service NFA obligations, according to Department of Budget and Management records. Critics argued that these resources could have been directed toward irrigation, mechanization, storage facilities, and rural infrastructure.

At the same time, rice smuggling remained a persistent problem. Department of Agriculture and USDA estimates suggested that hundreds of thousands of metric tons of rice entered the country outside official channels each year. Lost tariff revenues were estimated at billions of pesos annually.

Yet despite decades of intervention, the NFA remained only a small participant in the actual rice market. The agency purchased only around 3-5% of the country's annual palay harvest. Most farmers continued selling to private traders.

This created one of the greatest contradictions in Philippine rice policy. The government carried the financial burden of intervention, accumulated massive debt, and controlled imports for nearly half a century, yet private traders remained the dominant buyers of rice from Filipino farmers.

By 2018, the system was under severe strain. Import approvals required NFA Council authorization. Delays in approval contributed to declining buffer stocks. By the time imports arrived, prices had already surged. Rice disappeared from many NFA outlets. Commercial rice prices spiked. Inflation accelerated.

The events of 2018 did not create the crisis. They revealed the limits of a system that had been operating for nearly 47 years.

Who Paid? Taxpayers paid the debt. Consumers paid some of Asia's highest rice prices. Farmers remained dependent on traders despite decades of state intervention.

II. 1988: CARP — LAND REFORM WRITTEN BY LANDLORDS

Policy: Republic Act No. 6657, the Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program. Following the 1986 People Power Revolution, the new Constitution mandated agrarian reform as a cornerstone of social justice.

The objective was clear. Millions of Filipino farmers worked land they did not own. Agrarian reform sought to transfer ownership from large landowners to tenant farmers and agricultural workers.

Few would argue against the principle. Land ownership represented dignity, security, and opportunity. The question was not whether reform was necessary. The question was how it would be implemented.

The political context remains one of the most controversial aspects of CARP. A 1999 study by political scientist Jennifer Franco found that approximately 58% to 62% of House members in the 8th Congress came from landowning families or had direct agricultural business interests. Agrarian reform scholar Saturnino Borras Jr. later reached similar conclusions. Former DAR Secretary Ernesto Garilao would later describe the Congress that drafted CARP as being dominated by landowners.

This does not prove that lawmakers intended agrarian reform to fail. However, critics argue that it helps explain why the final law contained numerous compromises that balanced redistribution with protections for existing landowners.

Those compromises would shape Philippine agriculture for decades.

Among the most debated provisions was the retention limit. Landowners could legally retain five hectares, plus an additional three hectares for each qualified child. Critics argued that this provision enabled large estates to remain substantially intact through legal subdivision among heirs.

Another major issue involved CLOA titles. While beneficiaries received ownership rights, many discovered that ownership alone did not provide access to financing. For years, banks were reluctant to accept many agrarian reform titles as collateral. Without access to affordable credit, farmers struggled to invest in irrigation, machinery, fertilizer, and modern production methods.

Stock Distribution Options created another controversy. Rather than transferring land directly, corporations could distribute shares to workers. The most famous example was Hacienda Luisita. Although legal at the time, the arrangement remained controversial for decades before being voided by the Supreme Court in 2011.

The result was a pattern repeated across much of the countryside. Farmers received land but often lacked irrigation, financing, storage, equipment, and market access. Ownership changed. Productivity often did not.

CARP's Central Weakness: The reform focused on who owned the land. It was less successful in addressing whether that land could generate enough income and productivity to compete in a modern agricultural economy.

By 2022, average farm size had fallen to approximately 1.3 hectares. Ownership became more widespread, but farm fragmentation created new challenges. Smaller farms can be productive, but they often face greater difficulty accessing machinery, achieving economies of scale, and attracting investment.

The consequences became visible over time. Many beneficiaries leased, mortgaged, or sold their land after receiving it. Others remained trapped in cycles of low productivity and limited access to capital.

Agrarian reform succeeded in redistributing land. Whether it succeeded in creating competitive farms remains one of the most contested questions in Philippine agricultural policy.

Who Paid? Millions gained ownership. Many never received the irrigation, credit, infrastructure, and support services necessary to transform ownership into prosperity.

III. TALE OF TWO REFORMS: Vietnam vs Philippines, 1986-2019

While the Philippines pursued agrarian reform, another Southeast Asian nation was confronting a different challenge.

Vietnam entered the 1980s facing food shortages, economic crisis, and the lingering effects of war. Rice was not simply an agricultural issue. It was a national survival issue.

In 1986, Vietnam launched the Đổi Mới reforms, fundamentally restructuring its economy. Agricultural productivity became a national priority. Farmers received stronger land-use rights. Irrigation expanded. Rural infrastructure received major investment. Credit became more accessible.

The objective was straightforward: produce more rice at lower cost.

The Philippines, meanwhile, focused heavily on redistributing ownership through CARP. Vietnam focused heavily on improving productivity.

The divergence became increasingly visible during the 1990s.

Vietnam entered the WTO era with extensive irrigation systems, stronger farmer financing mechanisms, and rising yields. The Philippines entered with fragmented farms, incomplete irrigation networks, and continuing dependence on state intervention.

Perhaps the most significant difference was timing.

Vietnam liberalized trade after strengthening agricultural competitiveness. The Philippines maintained protection for decades while many structural weaknesses remained unresolved.

When the Philippines joined the World Trade Organization in 1995, it secured special treatment allowing rice import restrictions to remain in place. Extensions were later granted in 2005, 2012, and 2017.

In effect, the country received a 22-year warning.

Twenty-two years to expand irrigation.

Twenty-two years to improve farmer access to credit.

Twenty-two years to modernize agriculture.

The critical question is not whether twenty-two years was enough time.

The critical question is why so many of the same weaknesses identified in the 1990s remained unresolved by the time protection finally expired.

The 22-Year Question: The Philippines knew competition was coming. Why were farmers still unprepared when it finally arrived?

Supporters of the Philippine approach argue that social justice required land redistribution first. Critics argue that redistribution without productivity reforms left many farmers owning land but lacking the tools necessary to compete.

Vietnam pursued a different sequence. It focused heavily on productivity, irrigation, infrastructure, financing, and yields before fully exposing farmers to international competition.

The results became visible by the late 2010s.

Vietnam had become one of the world's largest rice exporters.

The Philippines was preparing to become one of its largest customers.

One country opened trade after strengthening farms. The other opened trade while still trying to fix them.

IV. 2019: RICE TARIFFICATION LAW — SURGERY WITHOUT REHAB

Policy: Republic Act No. 11203, Rice Tariffication Law. The law ended the NFA's import monopoly, allowed private sector rice imports, imposed tariffs on imported rice, and created the Rice Competitiveness Enhancement Fund (RCEF).

The Rice Tariffication Law is one of the most debated agricultural reforms in modern Philippine history.

Supporters argue that it lowered rice prices, improved supply stability, and finally complied with the country's WTO obligations. Critics argue that it exposed Filipino farmers to foreign competition before domestic agriculture was ready.

Both arguments contain elements of truth.

The law addressed a trade problem. It did not automatically solve the production problems that had accumulated over previous decades.

By 2019, the Philippines was entering a more open market while still carrying many of the structural weaknesses identified years earlier: fragmented farms, incomplete irrigation, limited access to affordable credit, and higher production costs than regional competitors.

Consumers benefited from increased supply. Rice prices declined from the extreme levels experienced during the 2018 crisis. Government also established the Rice Competitiveness Enhancement Fund, allocating approximately ₱10 billion annually for farm machinery, seed development, credit, and extension services.

However, modernization proved more complicated than simply distributing equipment.

In many areas, machinery arrived before irrigation systems. Some farms remained too fragmented for efficient mechanized use. Other farmers continued struggling with financing, logistics, and storage constraints.

The result was a familiar pattern. Trade reform moved faster than farm reform.

By 2024, imports reached record levels. The Philippines became the world's largest rice importer. Supporters argued that imports kept rice affordable. Critics argued that dependence on foreign supply had become a long-term vulnerability.

RTL's Core Dilemma: The law fixed trade restrictions in 2019. It could not instantly fix problems that had accumulated since 1988.

For many farmers, the issue was not competition itself. The issue was competing against producers operating with lower costs, better irrigation, larger farm sizes, and stronger support systems.

The debate over RTL ultimately reflects a deeper question: Should a country modernize first and liberalize later, or liberalize first and modernize later?

The Philippines chose the second path.

V. JUNE 2026: THE RISK TODAY

The rice story did not end with the Rice Tariffication Law.

Many of the same structural issues remain active today.

Average farm size remains approximately 1.3 hectares. Significant portions of rice land remain dependent on rainfall. Irrigation gaps persist. Farmers continue facing high input costs and limited access to affordable financing.

At the same time, dependence on imported rice has increased.

A large share of Philippine rice imports now originates from Vietnam. That dependence creates a new vulnerability. If major exporters face drought, export restrictions, geopolitical disruptions, or production shocks, the Philippines becomes exposed to risks beyond its control.

By mid-2026, concerns were already emerging. Fertilizer prices rose significantly following instability in the Middle East. PAGASA warned of continuing El Niño risks. Agricultural groups warned that harvests could be affected if adverse conditions persisted.

The country's rice problem therefore remains a balancing act between domestic production and external dependence.

Former Agriculture Secretary Manny Piñol summarized the concern bluntly when discussing food security risks:

"If ports are bombed, we surrender in 30 days."

Whether one agrees with the statement or not, it reflects a broader concern about food security in a country that increasingly relies on imported rice.

The 2026 Risk: The Philippines solved neither complete self-sufficiency nor complete competitiveness. Instead, it remains caught between domestic weaknesses and import dependence.

Farmers continue raising many of the same concerns heard decades ago: low farmgate prices, expensive inputs, inadequate irrigation, and slow implementation of long-promised reforms.

The frustration can often be summarized in a phrase frequently seen across public discussions:

"Puro salita, kulang sa gawa."
---

THE VERDICT: 3 DATES, 1 PATTERN

The story of Philippine rice can be traced through three dates.

In 1972, the country chose state control through the NFA. The result was a costly system focused on managing prices rather than improving productivity.

In 1988, the country redistributed land through CARP but failed to consistently provide the irrigation, credit, scale, and investment needed to make farms competitive. Political compromises produced millions of landowners, but many remained poor and undercapitalized.

In 2019, the Philippines opened its rice market through the Rice Tariffication Law. The problem was not the reform itself. The problem was entering competition after decades of unfinished agricultural modernization.

For nearly fifty years, the Philippines repeatedly treated a production problem as a trade problem. When prices rose, imports were adjusted. When shortages appeared, emergency purchases were made. Yet the underlying weaknesses remained: fragmented farms, inadequate irrigation, limited credit, and inconsistent long-term planning.

Vietnam chose a different path. It invested heavily in irrigation, infrastructure, and farm productivity before opening its market. One country became a major rice exporter. The other became the world's largest rice importer.

The difference was not geography. It was not climate. It was not the Filipino farmer.

The difference was governance.

Had agricultural modernization received the same long-term commitment given to political compromise, patronage, and short-term price management, the Philippines might have entered the 21st century alongside Vietnam as a major rice exporter rather than the world's largest rice importer.

The tragedy is not that the Philippines lacked solutions. The tragedy is that politics, vested interests, corruption, and policy inconsistency repeatedly prevented those solutions from being fully implemented.

The story of Philippine rice is ultimately not about agriculture.

It is about governance.

And for nearly five decades, governance failed to deliver the continuity necessary to make Filipino farmers globally competitive.
