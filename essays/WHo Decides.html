Who Decides Who Counts?

The Constitution Already Did: Objective Facts, Not Political Judgments.

⏱ 8 min read
Constitution • Senate • Quorum • Avelino
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
The Senate quorum debate is often framed as a dispute over numbers. It is not. The deeper constitutional question is who decides who counts. The Constitution begins with membership—an objective fact. Availability, by contrast, depends on judgment, interpretation, and circumstance. The difference is not arithmetic. It is constitutional design.

The Question Behind the Numbers

Membership Is an Objective Fact. Availability Is a Political Judgment.

A constitutional rule should be objective, predictable, and difficult to manipulate. Membership satisfies all three. Availability satisfies none of them.

A senator either holds office or does not. A seat is either occupied or vacant. Availability, however, depends on circumstances, interpretation, and ultimately someone's judgment. That is why the Constitution begins with membership rather than availability.

The question is simple:

Who decides who counts?

The Constitution provides a clear answer.

CONSTITUTIONAL TEXT
“A majority of each House shall constitute a quorum to do business.”

Article VI, Section 16(2) supplies the quorum requirement. Article VI, Section 2 identifies the Senate as a body composed of twenty-four senators.

The Constitution therefore provides both the institution and the denominator from which a majority is measured.

KEY PRINCIPLE
Before anyone asks who is available, the Constitution has already identified who belongs to the Senate.

The Constitutional Baseline

The constitutional calculation is straightforward. The Senate is composed of twenty-four senators. A majority is more than half of that membership. The quorum requirement therefore begins with a number that can be objectively determined before any political controversy arises.

This is where constitutional analysis often goes astray.

Some argue that quorum should be determined not by membership, but by availability. Under this view, senators who are unable to attend may be excluded from the denominator used to calculate a quorum.

That argument traces its roots to Avelino v. Cuenco.

The Constitution provides the rule.
Avelino provides the exception.
The distinction matters.

Avelino is not irrelevant. It remains part of Philippine jurisprudence. Any serious constitutional analysis must confront it. But jurisprudence should be read in light of constitutional principles, not the other way around.

Facts Versus Judgments

If membership is the constitutional baseline, then quorum is determined by an objective fact. A senator either holds office or does not. The denominator remains fixed, predictable, and resistant to manipulation.

If availability becomes the constitutional baseline, then someone must decide who counts and who does not.

  • Is a detained senator unavailable?
  • A senator abroad?
  • A senator in a hospital?
  • A senator refusing to attend?
  • A senator avoiding arrest?

The moment availability becomes the measure, constitutional certainty gives way to discretionary judgment.

The issue is not whether reasonable people can disagree about those situations. The issue is that someone must make the determination. Once constitutional requirements depend upon judgments about availability, the rule itself becomes vulnerable to interpretation, discretion, and ultimately politics.

Membership can be counted. Availability must be judged.

That is precisely why constitutions favor objective standards whenever possible.

When Exceptions Become Rules

Constitutions are written for periods of political disagreement and institutional conflict. They are designed to endure precisely because they rely on rules that can be applied consistently regardless of who happens to hold power.

This does not mean that Avelino should be ignored.

The real question is whether Avelino recognized a narrow exception under extraordinary circumstances or established a general rule that availability supersedes membership.

Those are not the same thing.

An exception is not limitless simply because it exists.

An exception remains an exception because it is confined to the circumstances that justified it.

Otherwise, the exception ceases to be exceptional and begins to replace the rule itself.

Who Decides Who Counts?

The Constitution begins with members.

Not available members.

Not convenient members.

Not members whose attendance can be secured on a particular day.

Members.

That is the constitutional baseline.

Any departure from it must be justified, not presumed, not declared by convenience, and not expanded through flexible interpretation alone.

The Constitution was not written to accommodate political expediency. It was written to restrain power.

Constitutions are not designed for the leaders we trust. They are designed for the leaders we do not.

Membership is an objective fact.

Availability can be weaponized.

The Constitution is built on facts.

Impunity prefers availability.
