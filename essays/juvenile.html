ANALYSIS • June 2026

THE PANGILINAN LAW FAILED TWICE: NO PREVENTION, UNDERFUNDED REHABILITATION

CICL cases fell 84% from 2017-2024 despite RA 9344, not because of it. We never invested in keeping kids out. We barely funded helping them after.

⏱ 8 min read
Juvenile Justice • RA 9344 • Bahay Pag-Asa • CICL
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
For two decades, the debate over the Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act focused on whether rehabilitation was too soft. The data suggests a different story. Juvenile crime fell dramatically when communities became safer, while rehabilitation remained unevenly funded and difficult to measure. The law failed twice: it neglected prevention and underfunded the rehabilitation system it mandated.

The 84% Drop RA 9344 Can't Claim

The Juvenile Justice and Welfare Council keeps the official count.

UP data scientist Rogelio Panao warned the gains could reverse. He's right to worry.

But look at when the drop happened. 2017-2024. The Duterte years. Anti-drug campaigns. Barangay interventions. Oplan Tokhang. Community rehab. Local crime-prevention drives.

No single program gets all the credit. But the timing is brutal: juvenile crime plummeted while government aggressively attacked the ecosystem that recruits kids.

  • Gangs using minors as couriers.
  • Drug networks using children as lookouts.
  • Addiction, dropouts, broken homes, streets where crime is the only employer.

When those pressures eased, fewer children crossed the line.

RA 9344 didn't drive that drop. Prevention did. And prevention wasn't in the law.

Failure #1: We Legislated the Cure and Ignored the Cause

RA 9344 is a rehabilitation framework. Diversion. Intervention. Reintegration. Every provision starts after arrest.

Congress spent two decades arguing age thresholds and detention rules. It spent almost no time funding the programs that keep kids from getting arrested: family intervention, school retention, anti-drug programs, barangay-level alternatives, policing of adult recruiters.

So the national strategy became about managing failure, not preventing it.

Bahay Pag-Asa is necessary. But it's triage, not policy.

We built the emergency room and called it a public health plan.

Failure #2: We Mandated Rehabilitation and Refused to Pay for It

RA 10630 was blunt: Every province and highly urbanized city must build a Bahay Pag-Asa. Target: 114.

13 years to meet the mandate. RA 10630 set the national government contribution at P5 million per center, with LGUs required to match it from their own funds. The rest was on them.

Result: Rehabilitation depends on your ZIP code. Makati hires psychologists. A 5th-class municipality can't afford a social worker.

We legislated equity. We funded inequality.

And even now, 118 is a building count, not a system.

  • Capacity: How many beds per 100,000 youth?
  • Staffing: How many licensed psychologists actually on payroll?
  • Outcomes: How many went back to school? How many reoffended?

You can't claim success when you don't measure it. After 20 years, there is still no national CICL recidivism database.

We built the centers. We never built the accountability.

We Punish the Kids. We Protect the Handlers.

Every RA 9344 hearing repeats the same line: “Syndicates use minors.”

If that's true, why is the debate about the age of the courier?

The real predators aren't 15. They're 35.

Adults who recruit children should face sentences that dwarf what the child gets. That's deterrence.

Instead, we chase symptoms and negotiate with the disease.

The Double Failure, In One Line

1. No prevention — We never seriously funded the community, family, and anti-drug programs that keep kids out of crime.

2. Underfunded rehabilitation — We mandated 114 centers, set the national share at P5M each, and waited 13 years to build them. Then never staffed them to standard.

CICL cases fell 84% anyway.

That drop came from outside RA 9344.

It came from pressure on the streets, not programs in the courtroom.

The Fix: Fund Both Ends. Measure Everything.

1. Prevention first

National funding for barangay-level intervention: anti-drug, family case management, school retention, TESDA for dropouts, alternatives to gangs. Plus targeted policing of adults who use minors.

2. Rehabilitation second — and real

Full national staffing grants for 118 BPAs. JJWC accreditation with teeth. Quarterly public dashboards: capacity, staff-to-child ratios, school reintegration, recidivism. No data, no budget.

3. Adults pay more

Heaviest penalties for using a minor in crime. Secure facilities for heinous offenses by minors — yes, they're needed.

The Verdict

RA 9344 didn't fail because it was compassionate.

It failed because it was incomplete.

Congress wrote a law for the courtroom and forgot the street corner.

Then it wrote a check for P5 million and called it a system.

The data is clear: The biggest gains happened when government made communities safer, not when it built more rehab centers.

We finally have 118 Bahay Pag-Asa.

Now build the foundation that keeps them empty.

Prevention first. Funded rehabilitation second. Measured outcomes always.
