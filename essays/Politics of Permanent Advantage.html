Politics of Permanent Advantage

Patrimonial Plunder by Politicians and the Press: Lee Kuan Yew Saw the Symptom. Paul Hutchcroft Explained the System.

⏱ 6 min read

Lee Kuan Yew Saw the Symptom. Paul Hutchcroft Explained the System.

In 1988, Lee Kuan Yew delivered a warning that many Filipinos dismissed as foreign arrogance.

“The Philippine press enjoyed all the freedoms, but they failed the Filipino people. A widely partisan press helped Filipino politicians to flood the marketplace of ideas with junk and confuse and befuddle the people...”

Most people remember the word junk.

They should pay closer attention to the word partisan.

Because a partisan press is not merely a press that takes sides.

It is a press that becomes invested in particular narratives.

Political scientist Paul Hutchcroft helps explain why. In his landmark study, Booty Capitalism: The Politics of Banking in the Philippines, Hutchcroft described a system of patrimonial plunder sustained not merely by corruption, but by the preservation of influence.

The bribery, kickbacks, stolen funds, and scandals are not the disease.

They are the symptoms.

The disease is a system built on preserving influence.

Influence becomes the currency.

Protection becomes the mechanism.

Corruption becomes the outcome.

This is why narratives matter.

In a system where influence must be preserved, narratives become valuable assets. They shape public attention. They determine which issues dominate the national conversation and which issues quietly disappear from it.

One of the most effective ways to strengthen a narrative is not to invent facts. It is to surround the narrative with authority.

Academics are quoted. Former officials are interviewed. Retired justices are consulted. Experts are invited onto television panels.

None of this is improper.

But none of it transforms interpretation into fact.

Soon the public begins to confuse authority with certainty.

But authority is not certainty.

And interpretation is not fact.

Facts are facts.

Interpretations are arguments about those facts.

Yet a partisan environment can gradually blur that distinction. One interpretation is repeated, reinforced, and amplified until it begins to appear as the only reasonable conclusion.

This does not require falsehood.

It requires repetition.

It requires selective emphasis.

It requires the continual reinforcement of a preferred narrative.

That is why citizens should become cautious whenever a narrative survives longer than the facts that originally supported it.

When new facts emerge, does the narrative change?

When contrary evidence appears, does the interpretation evolve?

Or does the same storyline continue, supported by a growing chorus of authoritative voices?

These questions matter because the preservation of influence often depends not on controlling what people think, but on controlling what they are encouraged to think about.

Lee Kuan Yew saw the symptom.

Paul Hutchcroft explained the system.

The lesson for Filipinos is not to reject the media.

Nor is it to blindly trust it.

The lesson is to become skeptical whenever a narrative is endlessly reinforced but rarely reexamined.

Because in a system built on preserving influence, the most powerful narrative is not always the one that convinces you of something.

Sometimes it is the one that convinces you that no further questions need to be asked.
