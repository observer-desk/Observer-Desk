The Senate Has a Leader. Does It Have a Legitimate One?

The debate over the June 3 quorum is no longer just political. It is a constitutional question about de facto authority, legal legitimacy, and who should ultimately decide.

⏱ 8 min reaad
Constitution • Senate • Governance
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
A functioning institution cannot wait indefinitely for legal certainty. The controversy surrounding the June 3 Senate quorum raises difficult questions about authority, legitimacy, and constitutional process. The Senate may already have a leader. Whether it has a legitimate one remains a question yet to be answered.

The Senate's Two Battles

The battle over the Senate leadership is being fought on two fronts: politics and law.

The political contest may already have produced a winner.

The legal contest has not.

Was there a valid quorum? Was the leadership change lawful? Was the Senate President properly removed?

These questions continue to dominate headlines and social media feeds. Supporters of both camps have confidently declared victory, each insisting that the law is on their side.

Yet amid the noise, a more immediate question confronts the institution itself:

Who should be leading the Senate while the legality of the quorum remains unsettled?

The answer may be uncomfortable for partisans on either side, but constitutional democracies have long recognized a practical reality:

Government cannot stop functioning simply because a legal dispute remains unresolved.

The Senate must still convene. Legislation must still be debated. Investigations must still be conducted. The nation's business cannot be suspended while lawyers argue over parliamentary procedure.

De Facto vs. Legitimate

This is where the distinction between a de facto leader and a legitimate leader becomes important.

The two are not always the same.

A legitimate leader is the individual who is legally entitled to the office after all constitutional and procedural questions have been settled.

A de facto leader is the individual who actually exercises the powers of the office while those questions remain unresolved.

In moments of political stability, the distinction hardly matters. The person exercising power is also the person legally entitled to it.

But during institutional disputes, the distinction becomes crucial.

And whether one likes it or not, the Senate today appears to be operating under the authority of a de facto leadership.

The new leadership controls the chamber floor. It presides over proceedings. Senate officers and employees recognize its authority. Official Senate business continues under its direction. Other government institutions deal with it as the Senate's representative.

These are not ideological observations. They are institutional facts.

To acknowledge these facts is not to declare the dispute settled.

It is merely to recognize reality.

The Doctrine That Keeps Government Running

Constitutional systems have long understood this reality. In fact, the law developed an entire doctrine around it.

This is precisely why the law developed what is known as the De Facto Officer Doctrine.

The doctrine exists because governments cannot operate on pause.

Imagine if every disputed election, appointment, or leadership change automatically rendered every official act void until a court rendered a final judgment. The machinery of government would grind to a halt.

The doctrine therefore serves a simple purpose: it protects the public from the consequences of governmental paralysis.

It does not determine who ultimately wins the legal argument. It merely ensures that government remains capable of governing while the argument takes place.

But the doctrine does not say that whoever seizes control automatically becomes lawful. Nor does it reward procedural shortcuts.

Who Should Decide?

Many Filipinos instinctively answer: the Supreme Court.

Not so fast.

Under our constitutional system, the Senate is not merely another government agency. It is a co-equal branch of government with authority over its own proceedings.

Traditionally, legislative chambers determine their own rules, their own procedures, and their own quorum requirements.

The Senate itself is therefore the first judge of its own proceedings.

Only when a genuine constitutional issue is raised does the possibility of Supreme Court review emerge. Even then, the Court must first determine whether the controversy is one it can properly decide.

The judiciary does not automatically become the referee every time lawmakers quarrel.

That does not mean judicial review is impossible. If constitutional requirements were violated, the Court may eventually step in.

Who Should File the Petition?

But before the Court can rule on the legality of the quorum, someone with the proper standing must first bring the challenge.

And if the June 3 proceedings were truly unconstitutional, time matters.

The strongest claim belongs to former Senate President Alan Peter Cayetano—the individual who claims he was unlawfully removed from office.

The longer such a challenge is delayed, the more the current leadership continues to exercise authority, conduct Senate business, and strengthen its position as the institution's de facto leadership.

If the quorum was indeed illegal, the proper response is not to debate it indefinitely in public.

It is to challenge it before the courts—and to do so without delay.

This distinction became especially important after the Supreme Court recently dismissed a challenge filed by a private citizen.

The Court did not rule that the quorum was valid. Neither did it rule that the leadership change was lawful.

It simply held that the petitioner lacked the standing necessary to bring the case.

That is a critical difference.

The Question Still Unanswered

And so the fundamental constitutional question remains unanswered.

Was the quorum legal?

Perhaps it was.

Perhaps it was not.

But until that issue is properly presented and authoritatively resolved, the Senate must continue functioning.

That is why constitutional systems distinguish between de facto authority and legal legitimacy.

The Senate controversy is one of those moments when the distinction matters.

For now, Senator Win Gatchalian appears to occupy the position of the Senate's de facto leader, exercising authority while the question of legitimacy remains unresolved.

Whether history and the law will ultimately recognize that leader as the legitimate one remains a question for the proper constitutional process to answer.

The Senate may already have a de facto leader.

What it does not yet have is a final legal answer.

Until that answer arrives, constitutional government demands two things at once: that institutions continue to function, and that the rule of law remain available to test whether those exercising power are truly entitled to it.

The Republic cannot afford paralysis.

Neither can it afford indifference to legality.
