Keeping the Human in Human Judgement

Using AI to challenge narratives, not replace judgment.

⏱ 6 min read
AI • Independent Thinking • Narratives
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
Artificial intelligence has become one of the most powerful research tools available to writers. Yet every generation inherits storytellers that help shape how people understand the world. This essay explores why I use AI, why I often argue with it, and why human judgment remains the most important safeguard against any narrative.

Let's address the obvious question. Yes, I use AI to help write my articles.

Some readers may assume that means a machine is doing the thinking for me. Others may wonder whether the opinions they read here are genuinely mine.

They're fair questions.

But let me be clear from the beginning: AI does not define me, nor does it shape my opinions.

My views were formed long before I ever typed my first prompt. They come from my experiences, observations, successes, failures, and the countless conversations I've had throughout my life.

AI wasn't there for any of them.

What AI does provide is a remarkably useful way to research ideas, explore competing arguments, organize information, and identify questions I may have overlooked.

In many ways, it has become one of the most useful research tools available to writers. Used responsibly, it can help writers spend less time searching for information and more time thinking about it.

The opinions, however, remain my own.

That reality did not begin with AI. Long before artificial intelligence existed, people were already relying on others to interpret the world for them.

AI does not define me nor shape my opinion.

The Newest Storyteller

For most of human history, people did not see the world directly. They saw it through storytellers.

Through newspapers, television networks, governments, corporations, advocacy groups, experts, and institutions that interpreted reality on their behalf.

Sometimes those interpretations were accurate.

Sometimes they were intentionally incomplete.

Sometimes they were wrong and framed the narrative.

"It doesn't have to be true; it just has to look like that."

Today, artificial intelligence has become the newest storyteller.

It can gather information faster than any human being. It can summarize complex issues in seconds. It can present arguments that sound convincing and authoritative. But AI inherits the same limitation as every storyteller before it:

It can only work with the information it is given. And the information it is given comes from people.

History shows that information has never been completely neutral. Every institution, publication, organization, and movement operates within its own assumptions, incentives, priorities, and perspectives. That does not automatically make them wrong.

But it does make them human.

It simply means they should not be treated as unquestionable.

Neither should AI.

I Argue With AI

One reason I know these articles are still mine is because I frequently disagree with the AI.

Sometimes it misunderstands the point I'm trying to make. Sometimes it takes an argument in a direction I reject. Sometimes it produces an answer that sounds reasonable but completely misses what I'm trying to say.

When that happens, I push back and ask more questions. I rewrite prompts. I challenge assumptions. The final article is rarely the AI's first answer. More often, it is the result of questioning, refining, correcting, and occasionally arguing.

That doesn't mean I automatically dismiss what AI tells me.

If an argument is supported by evidence, consistent with reliable data, and survives scrutiny, I have no problem accepting it.

If AI were doing the thinking for me, there would be nothing to argue about.

Good ideas should be judged by their merit, not by where they come from.

Reality Is the Ultimate Test

At the same time, data alone is not enough.

Research matters. Evidence matters. Facts matter.

But ultimately, reality matters most.

A report can be persuasive. A headline can be convincing. An institution can be respected. An AI can produce an answer that sounds authoritative.

Yet if those conclusions consistently fail to match observable reality, they deserve closer examination.

For me, the goal is not to reject information. The goal is to test information. That includes information from the media. It includes information from institutions. And it includes information from AI.

Reality does not care about narratives. Reality does not care about ideology. Reality simply is.

The most powerful safeguard against any narrative is the willingness to question it.

Conclusion

Artificial intelligence is an extraordinary tool.

It can help us find information, identify patterns, challenge assumptions, and understand complex issues faster than ever before.

That is exactly why I use it.

Not because I want a machine to think for me. But because I want to think more critically for myself.

Every generation inherits its storytellers. The challenge is not avoiding them. The challenge is remembering that no storyteller—whether a newspaper, a government, an institution, or an AI—can relieve us of the responsibility to think for ourselves.

That responsibility remains stubbornly human.

And perhaps that is exactly how it should be.

AI can help me write. It can help me research. It can help me organize information. It can even challenge my assumptions.

But it cannot live my life.

It cannot form my convictions.

And it cannot take responsibility for what I believe.

That responsibility remains mine. Because no matter how advanced technology becomes, the responsibility for deciding what is true, what matters, and what deserves belief still belongs to the human being behind the keyboard. And that responsibility cannot be outsourced.
