The Forgotten Sotto in Avelino v. Cuenco

The pro-12 quorum narrative keeps pointing to the senator who was excluded. It rarely discusses the senator who was absent, hospitalized, and still part of the story.

⏱ 5 min read
Constitution • Senate • Quorum
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
Every constitutional controversy eventually becomes a battle over facts. In the current Senate quorum dispute, much attention has focused on Avelino v. Cuenco and the senator the Supreme Court excluded from its computation. Yet buried inside the same case is a largely forgotten detail that may be just as important: another senator was absent, confined to a hospital, and still remained central to the Court's analysis.

Everyone pushing the 12-senator quorum narrative keeps talking about one man: Senator Tomas Confesor.

He was the senator in America.

He was the senator the Supreme Court excluded in Avelino v. Cuenco.

He is cited repeatedly as proof that the denominator can shrink.

But there is a forgotten—or perhaps intentionally neglected—detail in Avelino v. Cuenco.

There wasn't just one absent senator.

There were two.

The other absentee was Vicente Sotto.

Yes, a Sotto.

The grandfather of former Senate President Vicente "Tito" Sotto III.

On February 21, 1949, the Senate descended into chaos. A rebellion was underway against Senate President Jose Avelino. After hours of parliamentary maneuvering, Avelino adjourned the session and walked out with several allies. The senators who remained continued the proceedings and elected Mariano Cuenco as the new Senate President.

Avelino challenged the move before the Supreme Court, arguing that twelve senators could not possibly constitute a quorum in a twenty-four-member Senate.

The Supreme Court disagreed.

Confesor was in the United States and was excluded from the computation. That reduced the effective Senate membership from 24 to 23, making twelve senators enough for a quorum.

And this is where the pro-12 quorum narrative usually ends.

But while Confesor was in America, Vicente Sotto was in a hospital.

He was absent.

He could not answer the roll call.

He could not participate in the proceedings.

Yet the Court did not reduce the denominator beyond 23.

That forgotten detail is more than a historical curiosity.

The significance goes far beyond a footnote in a 1949 Supreme Court decision. The current Senate brouhaha is fundamentally a dispute over what determines the denominator: membership or availability. The Court went out of its way to explain why Tomas Confesor was excluded from the computation. It did not similarly reduce the denominator because Vicente Sotto was hospitalized. If absence alone were sufficient, the denominator would have fallen even further.

The distinction matters because availability is a moving target. It can change from day to day. It can be argued, interpreted, and even weaponized. Membership, by contrast, is an objective constitutional fact. A senator is a senator until the Constitution or the law says otherwise.

That is what makes the forgotten Sotto relevant today.

The pro-12 quorum narrative has spent days talking about the senator who was excluded. But before it asks the public to accept a denominator based on availability, it should first explain why the Court's attention was focused on Tomas Confesor's absence abroad—and not on Vicente Sotto's hospitalization at home.

That forgotten distinction may be the most important detail in Avelino v. Cuenco.
