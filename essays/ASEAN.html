Friends to All, Enemy to None

Has the Philippines Chosen Geopolitics Over Development?

⏱ 12 min read
Geopolitics • Economy • ASEAN
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
Three decades ago, the Philippines stood far ahead of Vietnam. In the early 1990s, the average Filipino earned several times more than the average Vietnamese. The Philippines enjoyed strong ties to the West, an English-speaking workforce, and one of the oldest alliances in Asia. Today, that advantage has largely disappeared. Vietnam has caught up in economic size, built a manufacturing powerhouse, and integrated itself into global supply chains. The question is not whether Vietnam became richer. The question is why the Philippines lost such a significant head start.

The Lost Lead

Three decades ago, the Philippines stood far ahead of Vietnam. In the early 1990s, the average Filipino earned several times more than the average Vietnamese. The Philippines enjoyed stronger institutions, closer integration with global markets, and a considerably higher standard of living.

Yet today, Vietnam has largely erased that advantage. In some measures, Vietnam's income per capita now rivals or exceeds that of the Philippines. More importantly, Vietnam has built one of the world's fastest-growing manufacturing economies.

The question is not whether Vietnam became richer.
The question is why the Philippines lost such a significant head start.

In 1990, the average Filipino earned roughly seven times more than the average Vietnamese. Today, that gap has almost completely disappeared.

This does not mean the Philippines failed. The country achieved significant progress over the last three decades. The more important observation is that Vietnam advanced much faster.

The challenge for policymakers is understanding what drove that divergence.

ASEAN's Economic Reality

The economic center of gravity in Asia has shifted dramatically over the last two decades. Regardless of political preference, one fact is increasingly difficult to ignore: China has become ASEAN's largest economic partner.

Trade between ASEAN and China now approaches one trillion dollars annually, making it one of the largest economic relationships in the world. China's trade with ASEAN is approximately double that of the United States.

China is now ASEAN's largest trading partner.
The economic reality of Asia has changed. The question is whether Philippine policy has adapted to that reality.

This transformation did not happen overnight. As China's economy expanded, so did its role in regional supply chains, manufacturing networks, infrastructure development, and investment flows.

Across Southeast Asia, Chinese capital has helped finance railways, highways, industrial parks, ports, logistics hubs, and energy projects. Countries throughout ASEAN increasingly view economic engagement with China not as a political statement, but as a practical component of national development.

Importantly, deeper economic engagement with China has not required abandoning relationships with other partners. Many ASEAN economies continue to maintain strong ties with the United States, Japan, South Korea, Europe, and regional neighbors.

The region's most successful economies have generally pursued a pragmatic approach: accept opportunities wherever they emerge and avoid becoming overly dependent on any single power.

This approach can be seen in Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia, and Singapore. These countries compete, cooperate, and trade with multiple partners simultaneously. Their objective is not geopolitical loyalty. Their objective is economic growth.

"The most successful economies in Southeast Asia did not choose one side. They chose development."

For the Philippines, this raises an important question. If ASEAN's economic future is increasingly interconnected with China, can the country afford to view China primarily through a security lens while its neighbors pursue broader economic engagement?

The Great Export Gap

While GDP figures provide one measure of economic performance, exports often reveal something far more important: a country's ability to produce, compete, and sell goods to the world.

This is where the contrast between the Philippines and several ASEAN neighbors becomes particularly striking.

Vietnam exports nearly five times more goods than the Philippines.
This is not merely a trade statistic. It represents factories, industrial parks, supply chains, logistics networks, and millions of manufacturing jobs built over decades.

Today, Vietnam exports approximately $397 billion worth of goods annually. Malaysia exports roughly $356 billion. Thailand exports around $300 billion. Indonesia exports more than $270 billion.

The Philippines, by comparison, exports approximately $85 billion in merchandise goods.

The difference is significant because exports are not simply numbers on a spreadsheet. They represent productive capacity.

Behind every exported electronic component, vehicle part, appliance, or manufactured product lies an entire economic ecosystem:

  • Factories and industrial parks
  • Ports and logistics infrastructure
  • Power generation capacity
  • Transportation networks
  • Skilled workers and engineers
  • Domestic supplier industries

Countries that successfully integrated into global manufacturing networks were able to create millions of jobs while steadily increasing incomes and productivity.

Vietnam provides perhaps the most dramatic example. Three decades ago, Vietnam was significantly poorer than the Philippines. Today, it has become one of the world's fastest-growing manufacturing hubs.

This transformation was not driven by a single foreign partner. Vietnam welcomed investment from South Korea, Japan, China, Taiwan, Europe, and the United States. It built industrial parks, modernized infrastructure, and aggressively integrated itself into global supply chains.

The result was not simply higher exports. The result was industrialization.

"Factories create exports. Exports create jobs. Jobs create prosperity. The countries that understood this built the manufacturing base that now drives their economies."

The Philippines chose a somewhat different path. The country developed a highly successful business process outsourcing industry and benefited greatly from remittances sent home by overseas Filipino workers.

These sectors contributed significantly to economic growth and helped millions of Filipino families. Yet neither created the large-scale manufacturing ecosystem that emerged in several neighboring ASEAN countries.

The question facing policymakers today is whether the Philippines can still close that gap. Can the country build the infrastructure, industrial capacity, and investment environment necessary to compete in an increasingly integrated Asian economy?

The answer may depend not only on domestic reforms, but also on how the Philippines chooses to engage with the major economic powers shaping the region.

The Vietnam Question

Whenever economic engagement with China is discussed in the Philippines, one argument inevitably emerges: territorial disputes make closer cooperation impossible.

At first glance, this argument appears reasonable. The Philippines continues to face tensions with China in the West Philippine Sea. Sovereignty concerns remain significant, and maritime incidents regularly dominate headlines.

Yet Vietnam presents a challenge to that assumption.

Vietnam maintains extensive trade with both China and the United States despite territorial disputes.
Its experience suggests that sovereignty disputes and economic cooperation are not necessarily mutually exclusive.

Vietnam has its own disputes with China in the South China Sea. The relationship between Hanoi and Beijing has experienced periods of tension, diplomatic friction, and competing territorial claims.

Yet despite these disagreements, China remains Vietnam's largest trading partner. At the same time, Vietnam maintains strong economic relations with the United States, Japan, South Korea, Europe, and the broader ASEAN region.

This reality raises an important question: if Vietnam can defend its sovereignty while maintaining extensive economic engagement with China, why is the Philippines often presented with a choice between security and economic cooperation?

The Vietnamese model is not perfect, nor should it be copied blindly. Vietnam has its own political system, economic structure, and historical circumstances.

However, Vietnam demonstrates something important: geopolitical disagreements do not automatically require economic disengagement.

Instead, Vietnam pursued a dual-track strategy.

  • Protect national interests and territorial claims.
  • Maintain economic engagement wherever beneficial.
  • Welcome investment from multiple partners.
  • Avoid excessive dependence on any single power.

The result was a remarkably pragmatic foreign policy. Vietnam strengthened trade with China while simultaneously expanding exports to the United States. It welcomed South Korean manufacturers, Japanese infrastructure investment, and Western capital.

Its success was not built on choosing China. Nor was it built on choosing America.

It was built on refusing to choose only one partner.

"Vietnam's lesson is not that disputes do not matter. The lesson is that disputes do not have to define an entire economic relationship."

For the Philippines, this distinction is critical. The debate should not be whether the country becomes pro-China or anti-China. Nor should it be whether the country remains pro-American.

The real question is whether economic opportunities are being evaluated on their developmental merits, or whether geopolitical considerations have become so dominant that potentially beneficial partnerships are dismissed before they are even explored.

The experience of Vietnam suggests that countries can simultaneously defend their sovereignty, maintain strategic partnerships, and pursue economic opportunities wherever they exist.

That may be the most important lesson of all.

The Influence Question

The discussion becomes more complicated when foreign policy enters the picture.

The Philippines is not simply another ASEAN country. For more than a century, it has maintained exceptionally close political, cultural, military, and institutional ties with the United States.

Those ties are not inherently negative. They have contributed to defense cooperation, educational exchanges, migration opportunities, and deep people-to-people connections.

Yet every strategic relationship carries influence. The question is whether that influence has shaped how the Philippines approaches China.

Influence is not control.
The more relevant question is whether strategic alignment has gradually narrowed the range of policy options considered politically acceptable.

This question became particularly visible during the Duterte administration.

For the first time in decades, Manila attempted to pursue a significantly warmer relationship with Beijing. The government sought infrastructure financing, economic cooperation, investment opportunities, and a more independent foreign policy.

Not all expectations were fulfilled. Not all announced projects materialized. Some initiatives moved slowly. Others never advanced beyond the planning stage.

Nevertheless, the period represented a noticeable shift in approach. Economic engagement with China became a higher priority.

Following the Duterte years, Philippine foreign policy moved back toward a stronger security alignment with the United States. Military cooperation expanded. Joint exercises increased. The security dimension of the U.S.-Philippines relationship became increasingly prominent.

At the same time, public discourse increasingly viewed China through the lens of strategic rivalry and maritime tensions.

This does not prove that Washington dictated Philippine policy. Such claims are difficult to substantiate and often oversimplify a far more complex reality.

However, it does raise a legitimate question:

Has the Philippines become so focused on managing security risks that it is overlooking potential economic opportunities?

Supporters of a more pragmatic foreign policy argue that the country's institutions often approach China primarily as a security challenge rather than an economic partner.

They point to the contrast with several ASEAN neighbors that maintain sovereignty disputes with China while continuing to pursue extensive trade, investment, and industrial cooperation.

Critics counter that security concerns cannot simply be separated from economics and that territorial disputes carry real national consequences.

Both perspectives deserve consideration.

The issue is not whether security concerns are legitimate. They are. The issue is whether those concerns have become so dominant that economic considerations receive insufficient attention.

The most successful economies in Southeast Asia rarely frame foreign policy as a choice between one major power and another. Instead, they seek to maximize opportunities from all sides while preserving strategic flexibility.

The Philippines has often spoken of an independent foreign policy. The challenge is whether that principle is being fully applied in practice.

If economic development is the primary objective, then every relationship should ultimately be evaluated by the same standard:

  • Does it create jobs?
  • Does it improve infrastructure?
  • Does it strengthen industry?
  • Does it increase national competitiveness?
  • Does it improve the lives of ordinary Filipinos?

These questions may be more important than geopolitical loyalty. And they may determine whether the Philippines can fully capitalize on its strategic position in an increasingly competitive Asia.

Beyond the Debt Trap Narrative

No discussion of Chinese investment is complete without addressing one of the most frequently cited concerns: the so-called debt trap narrative.

According to this view, developing countries risk becoming excessively indebted to China and may ultimately lose economic or political autonomy as a result.

The concern deserves serious consideration. Large infrastructure projects involve significant financial commitments, and poor decisions can burden future generations.

However, the debate is often presented in overly simplistic terms.

The critical question is not who finances a project.
The critical question is whether the project creates lasting economic value.

Around the world, infrastructure projects have produced mixed results regardless of who provided the financing. Some projects have generated growth, improved productivity, and expanded economic opportunities. Others have struggled because of poor planning, weak governance, or unrealistic expectations.

The nationality of the lender does not automatically determine the outcome.

A productive railway can connect economic centers. A modern port can increase exports. A power plant can reduce energy costs. An industrial park can attract manufacturers and create jobs.

When projects are well planned and integrated into a broader development strategy, infrastructure becomes an asset. When projects are poorly conceived, they become liabilities.

This distinction is important because it shifts the discussion away from ideology and toward governance.

Countries are not passive participants in development. Governments choose which projects to pursue. Officials negotiate financing terms. Institutions determine whether investments are linked to long-term economic objectives.

In other words, infrastructure does not fail simply because it was financed by China. Nor does it automatically succeed because it was financed by a Western institution.

Success depends largely on the quality of leadership, planning, and execution.

The experiences of both Africa and Southeast Asia illustrate this reality. Some countries have used external financing to modernize transportation networks, expand industrial capacity, and improve competitiveness. Others have struggled because projects lacked economic viability or were undermined by governance problems.

The lesson is not that financing should be accepted uncritically. The lesson is that financing should be evaluated based on national interest and economic return.

"A good project remains a good project regardless of who finances it. A bad project remains a bad project regardless of who finances it."

For the Philippines, the challenge is therefore not deciding whether Chinese investment is inherently good or inherently bad.

The challenge is building the institutional capacity to identify projects that genuinely improve productivity, strengthen industry, and contribute to long-term national development.

Ultimately, the success of any investment depends less on the nationality of the investor than on the ability of a country to transform opportunity into growth.

A Development-First Foreign Policy

The central question explored throughout this essay is not whether the Philippines should choose China over the United States.

That is a false choice.

The most successful economies in Southeast Asia did not become prosperous by choosing one major power and rejecting another. They became prosperous by pursuing opportunities wherever they existed.

Vietnam welcomed Chinese trade, South Korean manufacturing, Japanese infrastructure investment, and access to Western markets. Indonesia pursued a similar strategy. Malaysia did the same. Singapore perfected it.

Their objective was never geopolitical loyalty. Their objective was national development.

The Philippines faces a similar challenge today. As the global economy becomes increasingly interconnected and competitive, the country cannot afford to approach every relationship through a purely geopolitical lens.

Infrastructure remains insufficient. Manufacturing remains underdeveloped. Logistics costs remain high. Energy costs remain among the highest in the region. Industrial capacity still lags behind several ASEAN neighbors.

These are development problems. And development problems require development solutions.

The Philippines does not need to become pro-China.
Nor does it need to become anti-American.

It needs to become unapologetically pro-Philippines.

A truly independent foreign policy should not be measured by how closely the country aligns itself with one power or another. It should be measured by how effectively it advances Philippine interests.

Every foreign relationship should be evaluated through the same simple questions:

  • Will it create jobs?
  • Will it improve infrastructure?
  • Will it strengthen industry?
  • Will it attract investment?
  • Will it increase national competitiveness?
  • Will it improve the lives of ordinary Filipinos?

If the answer is yes, then the opportunity deserves serious consideration regardless of whether it comes from Beijing, Washington, Tokyo, Seoul, Brussels, or elsewhere.

This does not mean compromising sovereignty. It does not mean ignoring security concerns. It does not mean abandoning long-standing alliances.

It means recognizing that economic development and national security should complement one another rather than compete with one another.

The experience of ASEAN suggests that countries can defend their interests, maintain strategic partnerships, and still pursue economic opportunities wherever they exist.

The Philippines has the talent, the geography, the resources, and the strategic importance to do the same.

"The lesson from ASEAN's most successful economies is not that they chose China over America. The lesson is that they chose development over geopolitical loyalty."

Three decades ago, the Philippines stood ahead of many of its neighbors. Today, several have caught up or moved ahead.

The challenge facing the country is not deciding which great power to support. The challenge is ensuring that every partnership, every investment, and every policy decision serves a single purpose: the long-term prosperity of the Filipino people.

In the end, the most important question is not whether the Philippines is pro-China or pro-American.

The most important question is whether the Philippines is sufficiently pro-Philippines.
