P*tang Ina, Ako 'To!

He earns more than most Filipinos. So why did he lie to his son about ₱40? *A life of an average Juan.*

⏱ 8 min read
Cost of Living • Taxes • Working Class • Philippines
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
A fictionalized but relatable snapshot of an ordinary Filipino family in June 2026. Juan earns more than many workers, yet still finds himself counting every peso, sacrificing comfort, and struggling to provide even the smallest things for his child.

"P*tang Ina, Ako 'To!"

June 2026, Novaliches.

6:12 AM. Juan stumbles off the MRT. Everyone’s rushing to work. He’s just getting home from it.

Seven years of “How may I assist you today?” to people in Ohio at 3 AM. ₱38,427 a month. They call that middle class.

His phone buzzes.

PAYROLL CREDITED

For three seconds, he breathes.

Then he opens Maya.

₱32,373.74

He never touched the rest. Tax. SSS. PhilHealth. Pag-IBIG. ₱6,053.26 taken before he logged out. A toll gate inside his own body.

Another hour. EDSA to Novaliches. Standing. Sweating. Watching ₱14 jeepney signs blur past. That’s 44 hours a month. Unpaid. He works 176 hours. He lives 220 for the same money.

Home. Gate sticks. Rent’s ₱8,500. “Maintenance,” the landlord said in January. The only thing maintained is the price.

Maria’s at the stove. The flame is blue, then yellow, then nothing. She hits the tank. Ting. Empty.

She doesn’t say anything. She just looks at him. That’s worse.

She slides him monggo. Rice. She made ₱3,200 last month washing blood out of other people’s clothes. Cash. The only money that shows up whole.

The bills don’t ask. They collect. All figures June 2026.

Meralco: ₱3,287.11. Half isn’t electricity. It’s fees. Losses. Taxes on taxes.
Water: ₱614.00. Internet: ₱699.00 prepaid—ransom for overtime. No load, no extra shift.
Jeep: ₱14.00. MRT: ₱14.00. Until July 1. Then it doubles. Just like that.

Saturday. Grocery.

Maria’s hands shake over the calculator.

Rice 25kg: ₱1,300. Eggs. One whole chicken: ₱220 x 4 meals. Galunggong at ₱200/kg. Oil. Soap.

₱9,870.00 at the counter. Everything’s taxed. You pay to eat. You pay to clean the plate. You pay for the right to be tired.

Joshua reaches for the SkyFlakes. ₱20.00

Juan puts it back. “Next time, anak.”

Joshua doesn’t cry. He’s 8. He knows. That’s the part that breaks Juan.

Tuesday. The tank dies for real.

Maria walks it to the dealer, ₱1,080.00 in her hand. June 2026 rate. The guy drops it on her shoulder without a word. She’s 5’2”. The tank is 11kg. She walks home slow. Every step is ₱100.

Wednesday, 2:17 AM.

Joshua. 39.2°C. Burning.

Clinic: ₱500.00. Meds: ₱780.00. Friday the faucet snaps. Tubero: ₱450.00.

They pay. The slippers—toe out, sole flapping—stay. Next month. Maybe.

7:05 AM. Today. The moment.

Joshua’s by the door. Backpack on. Uniform washed in last week’s water.

“Dad, baon po.”

Juan checks the app. ₱41.63 left until Maria gets paid for the laundry tomorrow.

Forty-one pesos. Sixty-three centavos.

He looks at his son. At the hole in his slipper. At the ₱40 the kid needs so he won’t be the only one with no merienda.

And Juan lies.

“Anak, nakalimutan ko mag-withdraw. Bukas na lang. Okay?”

Joshua nods. “Opo.” Too fast. Like he was expecting it.

He walks to school. No baon. No slippers. Eight years old and already practicing hunger.

Juan closes the gate. Leans on it. And for the first time in seven years, he doesn’t go inside.

He stands there. A 33-year-old man. ₱38,427 a month. More than most.

Crying because he lied to his kid about ₱40.

11:48 PM. End of the month. June 30, 2026.

Joshua’s asleep. Maria’s folding uniforms for ₱200.00.

Juan opens his app. ₱7,361.63

That’s it. After 176 hours. After Maria’s back. After lying to his son.

₱7,361.63 Nine days. One ER visit. One “no slippers, no entry” from a teacher.

He does the math.

If he makes ₱38,427.00 and ends here, the guy on minimum—₱14,190.00 for 22 days at ₱645/day?

Rent: ₱5,000.00
Light and water: ₱1,800.00
Jeep: ₱1,232.00
Food: ₱7,000.00 if you kill your cravings
₱15,032.00

You’re ₱842.00 in debt before your kid learns to write his name.

That’s why Juan doesn’t post. He’s the buffer. The proof they use: “Kaya naman pala eh.”

He can. Barely.

Tomorrow at 9:30 PM, he becomes “John” again. “I completely understand your frustration, ma’am.”

In 15 days, another paycheck comes.

And the line will be there—tax, bills, rent, jeepney, school, gas, clinic—all of them, legal, smiling, hands out.

Payday isn’t when you get paid.

Payday is when you find out what’s left of you.

And if this is “more than most”—
If ₱38,427.00 means lying to your son about ₱40.00 because the country took the rest—

Then f*ck your “resilience.”

Juan is tired of hearing that word. Resilience. Politicians love it. They praise it every time prices rise, every time another tax appears, every time another fee gets attached to a bill. As if resilience were a reward. As if resilience could pay rent. As if resilience could buy medicine. As if resilience could put ₱40 into a child's hand before school.

Juan fought fair and square. Maria fought fair and square. They worked. They paid taxes. They followed the rules. They contributed to every system they were told would help build a better country. Yet somehow the people carrying the heaviest load always seem to have the least left at the end of the month. Meanwhile corruption scandals come and go, politicians grow richer, government offices grow larger, and the bill always finds its way back to people like Juan.

Tomorrow morning, somewhere in the Philippines, another father will say, “Bukas na lang, anak.” Not because he is lazy. Not because he refuses to work. But because ordinary Filipinos are fighting the fight honestly while too many institutions have learned how to take without giving back. The government will call that resilience. Juan has another word for it...

P*tangina, ikaw din ba 'to?
