The Prestige Scam

How Awards like NOBEL Stopped Signaling Excellence — and Started Manufacturing Legitimacy

⏱ 8 min read
Politics • Academia • Media • Institutions
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
From Nobel Peace Prizes and honorary doctorates to Ramon Magsaysay Awards and corporate recognitions, honors once served as society's clearest signals of excellence. Today, however, many awards have become battlegrounds over politics, ideology, and legitimacy. As institutions increasingly find themselves defending not only recipients but the meaning of recognition itself, a deeper question emerges: are awards still celebrating achievement, or are they manufacturing authority?

The Nobel: From Conscience to Controversy

No prize better illustrates the shift than the Nobel Peace Prize.

In 2009, Barack Obama received it nine months into his presidency. Supporters saw a nod to renewed diplomacy. Critics saw an award for promise, not peace. In 2019, Abiy Ahmed was lauded for ending war with Eritrea — only for Ethiopia to descend into conflict months later. In 2021, Maria Ressa and Dmitry Muratov were honored for defending free expression, a choice admirers called courageous and detractors viewed as an endorsement of a particular ideological and political narrative.

Even non-recipients shape the debate. Donald Trump's repeated nominations, and his supporters' claims that the Abraham Accords merited recognition, became a referendum on the Committee itself.

The pattern is clear: discussion no longer centers on what the laureate did. It centers on what the institution meant to say. The prize has become a geopolitical signal.

Universities: Lending the Robe

If the Nobel Committee wields moral authority, universities wield intellectual authority. That makes honorary doctorates uniquely combustible.

Unlike earned degrees, honorary ones are pure symbolism — a university attaching its reputation to a public figure. Historically, recipients were post-career titans whose legacies had hardened. Today, the honors go to active political actors, and the backlash is immediate.

In the Philippines, former Vice President Leni Robredo's five honorary doctorates are cited by supporters as tribute to public service; by critics as academic partisanship. In 2025, Ateneo's doctorate for former Senator Leila de Lima drew similar lines. The same dynamic engulfed the University of the Philippines in 2017, when a proposed honor for then-President Rodrigo Duterte sparked protests from students, faculty, and alumni before he ultimately declined it.

The question isn't whether these figures deserve respect. It's what a university does when it confers it. An honorary degree is not a CV line. It is an institutional endorsement. And in polarized societies, that endorsement is currency.

The Magsaysay Dilemma

The same tension exists in humanitarian and civic awards.

In 2025, Fr. Flavie Villanueva received the Ramon Magsaysay Award for his work with families affected by the Philippines' anti-drug campaign. Admirers saw the recognition as a tribute to compassion, service, and human dignity. Critics viewed the award through the broader political debate surrounding the drug war itself.

The controversy was not fundamentally about Fr. Villanueva.

It was about the meaning of the award.

Like the Nobel Peace Prize, the Ramon Magsaysay Award carries a reputation built over decades. When such an institution honors an individual, it inevitably communicates more than admiration for a person's work. It also signals which values, causes, and narratives deserve public recognition.

Whether one agrees with the selection is secondary. The point is that even humanitarian awards increasingly find themselves drawn into larger political debates. Recognition becomes inseparable from interpretation.

The Business of Laurels

Nowhere is the monetization of prestige clearer than in corporate awards.

"Best Workplace," "Top Innovator," "Excellence in Leadership" — some are rigorous, peer-reviewed benchmarks. Others are "pay-to-play": nomination fees, gala tables, and sponsorship packages blur the line between vetting and vending.

The harm isn't that every winner is undeserving. It's that outsiders can't tell the difference. A trophy on a lobby wall implies independent validation. When the process is opaque, prestige becomes a product, recognition a service, and legitimacy something you can invoice.

The incentive is structural: the more trusted the institution, the more valuable its seal. The more valuable the seal, the greater the temptation to rent it out.

When Honor Inflates

Economists warn of currency inflation — when money grows faster than value. Prestige inflates the same way.

An award's worth rests on scarcity and trust: the belief that it is selective, earned, and independent of patronage. Each time an honor doubles as political messaging, public relations, or fundraising, that belief erodes. Not in one scandal, but in a thousand small doubts.

No single dubious Nobel, doctorate, or corporate laurel destroys credibility. Cumulatively, they do. Institutions end up defending not the recipient, but the award itself. That is the tell: when you have to argue that a prize still means something, the inflation is already underway.

Once that belief frays, the medal remains. The meaning doesn't.

This is not about whether Ressa, Obama, Trump, Robredo, de Lima, Duterte, or Fr. Flavie Villanueva deserved their honors. Those arguments will outlive us. The deeper issue is institutional: are gatekeepers protecting the scarcity that gives prestige meaning?

Awards work because trust works. A Nobel, a doctorate, a Magsaysay, or a boardroom trophy commands deference only if the public believes it wasn't bought, bartered, or rushed.

And unlike prizes, trust is not awarded annually. Once spent, it is brutally hard to earn back.
