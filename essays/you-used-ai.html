"You Used AI": The New Way to Dismiss an Argument

Political correctness, online gatekeeping, and why ideas should be judged by their merits—not by the tools used to express them.

⏱ 8 min read
AI • Free Speech • Public Discourse
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
The accusation "You used AI" is increasingly used as a way to dismiss opinions without addressing their substance. But should arguments be judged by the tools used to express them—or by the evidence and reasoning they contain?

If you've spent any time in online discussions recently, you've probably seen it happen.

Someone writes a detailed response. The points are organized. The grammar is clean. The argument is coherent.

Then someone replies:

"You used AI."

The implication is usually clear: because artificial intelligence helped compose the response, the person's opinion is somehow less valid.

What's striking is that this accusation often arrives without addressing a single point that was actually made.

The discussion shifts from the argument to the tool.

In many cases, "You used AI" has become the modern equivalent of saying:

"Your opinion doesn't count."

Political Correctness and the Fear of Saying the Wrong Thing

Over the past decade, public discourse has become increasingly cautious.

Political correctness, at its best, encourages civility and respect. Most reasonable people would agree that discussions are more productive when participants avoid unnecessary insults and personal attacks.

However, there is a difference between encouraging respectful discussion and creating an environment where people become afraid to express themselves imperfectly.

A poorly worded sentence can be taken out of context, screenshotted, mocked, or interpreted in the least charitable way possible.

Many people have learned that expressing an unpopular opinion can carry consequences far beyond the discussion itself.

As a result, countless individuals spend more time worrying about how they sound than whether their ideas deserve consideration.

We claim to value open discussion, yet many people hesitate to participate because they fear being judged for how they express themselves rather than what they are trying to say.

Most people are not using AI to think for them. They are using AI to help express what they already think.

Why People Turn to AI

Contrary to popular assumptions, most people are not asking AI what to believe.

They are asking AI how to communicate their beliefs more effectively.

Someone may have a valid argument but struggle to organize it.

Someone may have evidence but lack confidence in their writing ability.

Someone may speak English as a second language and want assistance expressing themselves clearly.

Others simply want help refining a rough draft before sharing it publicly.

AI helps bridge those gaps.

In that sense, AI is not fundamentally different from a spell checker, grammar software, search engine, editor, translator, or even a trusted friend who reviews a draft before publication.

The ideas still belong to the user.

The responsibility still belongs to the user.

The conclusions still belong to the user.

When the Tool Becomes the Target

Imagine spending twenty minutes researching a topic.

You gather sources.

You organize your thoughts.

You draft a response.

You then use AI to improve readability and fix grammatical mistakes.

Instead of responding to your evidence or reasoning, someone replies:

"Nice AI response."

The discussion immediately changes.

No longer are people debating the argument.

Now they are debating the tool.

This is a classic example of judging a claim by its origin rather than by its merits.

A strong argument remains strong even if AI helped improve its wording.

A strong argument remains strong even if AI helped improve its wording.

The New Form of Gatekeeping

One aspect of the AI debate receives surprisingly little attention.

Not everyone begins with the same ability to communicate.

Some people are naturally gifted writers.

Others struggle to organize their thoughts into coherent paragraphs.

Some speak multiple languages and communicate in English only as a secondary language.

Others possess valuable experience and insights but lack confidence in their writing ability.

Historically, those people were often disadvantaged in public discussions.

Their ideas could be ignored simply because they were not presented elegantly.

AI changes that.

For the first time, a person with average writing skills can present ideas with a level of clarity that was previously available only to strong writers, journalists, academics, and professional communicators.

"You needed AI to write that."

But what exactly is being criticized?

The argument?

Or the fact that someone needed assistance expressing it?

If someone wears glasses, we do not accuse them of cheating at seeing.

If someone uses a calculator, we do not accuse them of cheating at mathematics.

If someone uses translation software, we do not accuse them of cheating at communication.

Yet many people react differently when AI is involved.

Intelligence Is Not Measured by Writing Ability

Perhaps the most troubling assumption behind AI criticism is the belief that writing ability and intelligence are the same thing.

They are not.

Writing is a skill.

Intelligence is much broader.

A person can possess excellent reasoning skills and still struggle to write clearly.

Likewise, someone can write beautifully while offering little substance.

History is filled with brilliant individuals who relied on editors, assistants, translators, researchers, and collaborators.

Yet when AI enters the conversation, some suddenly assume assistance is evidence of intellectual weakness.

Writing is a skill. Intelligence is much broader.

This attitude risks creating a new form of elitism.

Instead of evaluating ideas on their merits, we begin evaluating people based on their ability to phrase those ideas.

That is a mistake.

The Real Test of an Argument

The real test of an argument has not changed.

Are the facts accurate?

Is the reasoning sound?

Does the evidence support the conclusion?

These questions mattered before AI existed.

They matter now.

If an AI-assisted comment contains factual errors, challenge the facts.

If the reasoning is flawed, expose the flaws.

If the evidence is weak, question the evidence.

But dismissing an argument solely because AI was involved is not critical thinking.

It is simply another way of avoiding the discussion.

Conclusion

For years, society encouraged people to find their voice.

Now that technology allows more people to express themselves clearly, some have decided those voices no longer count if assistance was involved.

That is not a defense of critical thinking.

It is simply a new form of gatekeeping.

Political correctness has made many people more cautious about expressing controversial opinions. AI has emerged as a tool that helps ordinary individuals communicate their thoughts with greater clarity and confidence.

Yet instead of evaluating what is being said, many discussions now focus on how it was written.

A society that values free inquiry should judge arguments by their logic, evidence, and merits—not by whether a grammar checker, editor, researcher, translator, or AI assistant helped organize the words.

The next time someone says, "You used AI," ask a simple question: Does that make the argument wrong?

If the answer is no, then the discussion should return to where it belonged in the first place:

The ideas themselves.
