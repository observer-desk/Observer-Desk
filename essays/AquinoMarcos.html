Noynoy Aquino and Bongbong Marcos Are the Same... In Some Ways

Their supporters see them as opposites. Yet a closer examination of their rise to power, governance records, and political coalitions reveals striking similarities.

⏱ 12 min read
Politics • Governance • Philippines
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
Philippine politics often presents Aquino and Marcos as opposing political traditions. Yet beyond the narratives, both presidencies reveal recurring patterns of dynastic politics, political timing, governance failures, corruption controversies, and elite political continuity.

Both Inherited Powerful Political Brands

The Philippines remains heavily influenced by political inheritance.

Aquino and Marcos entered national politics carrying two of the most recognizable surnames in the country. Both were also namesakes of nationally known fathers. Benigno Aquino III carried the name of Benigno "Ninoy" Aquino Jr., while Ferdinand Marcos Jr. carried the name of Ferdinand Marcos Sr.

Long before either sought the presidency, their names already carried history, symbolism, loyal constituencies, and deeply entrenched political narratives.

For supporters, those legacies represented continuity. For critics, they reflected the enduring strength of dynastic politics.

Either way, neither began the race as an ordinary politician.

Both Reached the Presidency on Political Tides Larger Than Themselves

A famous surname alone does not guarantee a presidential victory.

Both Aquino and Marcos benefited from political moments that transformed the electoral landscape.

For Aquino, the turning point was the death of former President Corazon Aquino in 2009. The national outpouring of sympathy that followed transformed a candidacy that had previously seemed unlikely into a powerful political movement.

For Marcos, the decisive factor was a different political tide. His alliance with Sara Duterte allowed him to consolidate much of the coalition built during the presidency of Rodrigo Duterte.

Before becoming president, both men served in the Senate. Yet neither entered the presidential race with a defining personal achievement that alone could explain his rise to Malacañang.

The circumstances differed. The pattern was remarkably similar.

Both entered the presidency as beneficiaries of forces larger than themselves.

Both Struggled With Highly Visible Governance Problems

Governments are often judged not by abstract policies but by problems citizens encounter in everyday life.

For Aquino, transportation became a recurring political liability. MRT-3 suffered breakdowns, overcrowding, maintenance controversies, and chronic service disruptions affecting millions of commuters. NAIA became associated with congestion, delays, aging facilities, and operational inefficiencies.

Aquino also publicly tied himself to transportation promises.

"Pag hindi ho nangyari ito, nandyan ho si Secretary Abaya na nangangasiwa ng proyektong ito, dalawa na kaming magpapasagasa siguro sa tren."

The statement reflected the administration's confidence that the project would proceed successfully. Critics later revisited the remark when the project remained unfinished at the end of Aquino's term.

Marcos has faced a different but equally visible challenge.

Rather than train failures dominating headlines, recurring floods have become one of the defining governance issues of his administration. In successive State of the Nation Addresses, Marcos highlighted hundreds of flood-control projects as evidence of government action and infrastructure investment.

Yet every major flooding event has raised the same question: if billions have already been spent and thousands of projects have been completed, why do so many communities remain vulnerable?

The resulting scrutiny has fueled allegations of waste, poor implementation, ghost projects, and corruption in flood-control spending.

For Aquino, the symbol became failing trains and congested airports. For Marcos, it risks becoming flood-control systems that many citizens increasingly question.

Both Were Judged During National Crises

Presidents are often remembered most clearly during moments of crisis.

For Aquino, Typhoon Yolanda became the defining challenge of his presidency. The scale of the disaster generated lasting questions about preparedness, coordination, and government response.

For Marcos, severe flooding has repeatedly produced similar questions regarding infrastructure effectiveness, disaster preparedness, and government readiness.

Natural disasters are not created by presidents. But citizens inevitably judge whether governments were prepared for them.

Both Administrations Were Shadowed by Corruption Controversies

National crises often expose deeper questions about governance, accountability, and public trust.

Under Aquino, public debate was repeatedly consumed by controversies surrounding the Disbursement Acceleration Program (DAP), the broader fallout from the Napoles pork barrel scandal, and later the political repercussions of Dengvaxia.

Under Marcos, questions surrounding budget reallocations, PhilHealth funding, flood-control spending, and government transparency have generated similar scrutiny.

The specifics differ. The pattern does not.

In both administrations, controversies involving public resources evolved into broader debates about accountability, oversight, and whether government institutions were serving the public interest.

Neither president was personally accused in every controversy associated with his administration. Yet both found their governments repeatedly defending how public funds were managed and whether sufficient safeguards existed against abuse, waste, or corruption.

Both Faced Perceptions of Being Out of Touch

Perhaps the most politically damaging similarity is that both men struggled with perceptions that they were disconnected from ordinary Filipinos.

Aquino came from one of the country's most influential political families. Early in his presidency, controversy erupted over his purchase of a Porsche sports car.

The perception deepened after the SAF 44 tragedy. During an interaction with the widow of one of the fallen officers, Aquino reportedly remarked:

"Pareho lang tayo. Namatayan din ako ng tatay."

Supporters viewed the statement as an attempt at empathy. Critics viewed it as a revealing moment of disconnect.

Marcos has faced a different version of the same criticism. Images of lavish events, luxury, high-profile social gatherings, and the lifestyle associated with the country's political elite have often contrasted sharply with the economic realities confronting many Filipinos.

He also faced criticism after remarking:

"If I wasn't president, I might be out on the streets with them. Of course, they are enraged. Of course, they are angry. I'm angry. We should all be angry."

The statement was intended to express solidarity. Critics instead saw irony in a sitting president speaking as though he were observing the problem rather than occupying the office responsible for addressing it.

Different moments. Different circumstances. The same criticism.

Neither Built a Defining Infrastructure Legacy

Infrastructure is often how presidents leave a visible mark on a nation.

Yet neither Aquino nor Marcos appears destined to be remembered primarily for a transformative infrastructure legacy.

Aquino's administration was frequently criticized for delays in project implementation despite strong economic growth and healthy government finances.

Marcos inherited much of the momentum generated by the Duterte administration's Build, Build, Build program. While his government has continued and expanded many projects, critics argue that his presidency has yet to produce a landmark infrastructure initiative uniquely identified with his administration.

In both cases, the presidents became associated more with managing inherited projects than creating defining ones.

The Irony of Philippine Politics

Perhaps the most surprising similarity emerged after the elections were over.

Many of the political figures, institutions, personalities, commentators, and interest groups that once rallied behind Aquino eventually found themselves cooperating with, supporting, or becoming part of the broader coalition surrounding Marcos.

The irony is difficult to ignore.

The same political establishment that helped elevate Aquino to power now contains many of the figures who support, cooperate with, or benefit from the administration of Marcos.

That does not mean Aquino and Marcos are identical. Their ideologies, historical circumstances, and political narratives remain different.

But when viewed through the lens of governance, both inherited powerful political brands. Both rode political tides larger than themselves. Both struggled with visible governance failures. Both were tested by national crises. Both presided over administrations plagued by corruption controversies. Both faced accusations of being out of touch with ordinary Filipinos. And neither left behind a defining infrastructure legacy that clearly belonged to them alone.

Perhaps the most surprising similarity of all is that many of the people who once championed Aquino while opposing Marcos eventually found themselves supporting both.

By all these comparisons, as leaders of the country, Noynoy Aquino and Bongbong Marcos are the same...

In many ways.
