When Congress Holds the Purse, Who Watches the Spenders?

A PGMN investigation into Martin Romualdez and the House budget asks: How much visibility do we get into the chamber that writes the national budget?

⏱ 8 min read
Congress • Budget • Transparency • Martin Romualdez
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
A recent Peanut Gallery Media Network (PGMN) investigation hosted by CJ Hirro examines the rapid growth of the House of Representatives' budget under former Speaker Martin Romualdez, raising questions about transparency, accountability, and how Congress manages its own spending. While the allegations discussed remain unadjudicated, the episode challenges viewers to consider whether the institution responsible for writing the national budget is providing the public with sufficient visibility into how billions of pesos are allocated and spent.

One billion pesos.

In CJ Hirro's PGMN deep-dive, that stops being a number on a slide. It's 1,000 classrooms for towns that have waited years. It's dialysis, chemo, and insulin for families lining up at public hospitals. It's rice, eggs, and a little dignity for households stretching every paycheck.

Then Hirro turns the camera inward:

What happens when billions move inside the chamber that writes the national budget?

The nearly three-hour episode centers on former Speaker Martin Romualdez. But PGMN argues the issue is structural: how Congress budgets for itself, and how hard that spending is for the public to follow.

Hirro doesn't claim the increases were illegal. Her case is that control over the House's internal funds became more centralized and less legible during Romualdez's speakership. In her telling, that makes this a story about how power operates, not just how money moves.

Whether you agree with her or not, the episode forces a question that outlasts any one official.

The House's Budget Jump

It starts on paper.

Documents cited in the episode show the House of Representatives' allocation rising from about ₱21.6 billion in 2022 to roughly ₱33.7 billion by 2025 — a ₱12.1 billion increase in three years.

PGMN notes that part of the growth came through adjustments beyond the National Expenditure Program submitted by the Executive. The episode highlights Maintenance and Other Operating Expenses — the MOOE line that covers office supplies, travel, and activities that aren't easily itemized.

Context is the point.

  • Who chaired key committees.
  • Who set internal priorities.
  • Who signed off when the House funded itself.

So Hirro asks:

If Congress holds the purse strings, why did its own share grow this much while schools and hospitals reported shortfalls?

The COA Fight

The episode rewinds to a long-running dispute: the Commission on Audit's complaints about how Congress liquidates funds.

The issue is simple — can certifications substitute for receipts?

Defenders say legislative work doesn't always leave a paper trail like procurement does.

Critics say taxpayer money should be traceable, regardless of which branch spends it.

For PGMN, the risk isn't just accounting procedure. Weak traceability, the episode argues, lets large sums pass through with less scrutiny than Congress requires of agencies.

If the House can demand every OR from an agency, shouldn't its own expenditures be just as readable to the public?

This isn't only about legality.

It's about whether ordinary citizens can see where the money went without hiring a lawyer.

The Committee You Don't See on TV

Headlines usually gather at the Appropriations Committee.

PGMN points elsewhere: the House Accounts Committee.

That panel handles the chamber's internal budget — salaries, equipment, food, repairs, construction, bonuses.

During the period Hirro examines, it was chaired by Tingog Party-list Rep. Yedda Marie Romualdez, wife of the then-Speaker.

The episode's argument is blunt:

You can't discuss House spending without looking at who ran the controls. Follow the mechanisms, not just the totals.

PGMN contends the Accounts Committee deserves more public attention.

Appropriations tells the country where money goes.

Accounts shapes how Congress spends on Congress.

Romualdez's Position

Martin Romualdez has denied wrongdoing.

His stance hasn't shifted:

  • Congress doesn't implement projects.
  • It doesn't procure.
  • It doesn't build.
  • Executive agencies do.

Supporters say that makes him an easy target when scandals surface.

Critics say Congress doesn't pour concrete, but it authorizes and oversees the budgets that make it possible.

PGMN refuses to separate leadership from outcomes.

Even without signing contracts, the episode asks who influenced the systems and budget moves that enabled the spending.

None of this has been settled in court.

The narratives conflict, and the legal questions remain open.

Bigger Than One Speaker

The episode's core isn't about a surname.

It's about design.

Even lawful spending erodes trust if the system is opaque.

Citizens shouldn't need to act like auditors to track public funds.

The point isn't just to catch wrongdoing.

It's to let people understand the choices made in their name.

Not only "what happened," but "why is it so hard to see?"

Why This Lands Now

Budgets are trade-offs.

Every peso allocated here is a peso not used elsewhere.

When Congress increases its own funding, the public is entitled to ask what was deprioritized.

That question cuts deeper when hospitals and schools say they're competing for scraps.

What level of visibility should we expect from the branch that controls the money?

That standard doesn't depend on who sits as Speaker or who's in Malacañang.

It applies to every Congress, every year.

The Question That Lingers

Hirro assembles documents, hearing clips, and testimony into one prompt:

If the House can raise its own budget, shift funds between categories, and liquidate under rules most Filipinos don't know, what does real accountability look like?

That reaches beyond Martin Romualdez and party lines.

It's about how institutions keep faith with the public.

Because when the institution that writes the national budget is also spending billions of pesos in public funds, the final check is still the Filipino people.

An informed public asks better questions.

You don't have to agree with CJ Hirro on everything to agree the discussion is overdue.

This isn't only about one person's term as Speaker.

It's about whether we get clear, accessible accounting from those who hold our money.

Trust isn't built on assurances.

It's built on records.

The questions PGMN raised warrant scrutiny, debate, and answers from the people entrusted with the purse.
