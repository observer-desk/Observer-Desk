“Not My Job to Write Lesson Plans”

A Senate hearing, a viral backlash, and the collapse of Risa Hontiveros' Adolescent Pregnancy Bill

⏱ 10 min read
Education • Senate • Policy
Observer Desk
Think Beyond the Narrative
The Prevention of Adolescent Pregnancy Act was intended to address teenage pregnancy and rising HIV infections among Filipino youth. Instead, it became the center of a national debate over parental authority, curriculum control, and the role of international standards in public education. What began as a public health proposal ultimately helped reshape the country's approach to sexuality education.

The question was no longer what SB 1979 explicitly said. The question had become what people believed it would eventually allow.

The fight started with a video.

On January 10, Project Dalisay released a 12-minute presentation titled Unmasking the Perils of CSE. The video warned parents that an international framework promoted by organizations such as UNESCO, UNFPA, and UNICEF would expose children to sexual concepts at increasingly younger ages.

By the end, viewers had heard references to masturbation, sexual pleasure, and sexual rights.

The message was simple: this was coming to Philippine classrooms.

The presentation spread rapidly across social media, transforming what had been a relatively obscure legislative proposal into a national controversy. Within weeks, a bill that had spent years moving through Congress was in crisis.

At the center of the controversy was Senate Bill 1979, the Prevention of Adolescent Pregnancy Act, authored by Senator Risa Hontiveros.

And at the center of the bill's defense was a statement that turned out to be legally persuasive—but ultimately insufficient.

“Not My Job to Write Lesson Plans”

Hontiveros argued that the explicit terms critics were highlighting were not contained in the bill and were not mandated for classroom instruction. The bill only established the framework, she said. As for the curriculum itself, she maintained that she was not going to write the lesson plans.

That was DepEd's job.

The Bill That Wasn't About Masturbation

The Prevention of Adolescent Pregnancy Act was designed to address a real problem.

The Philippines continues to record one of the highest adolescent birth rates in Southeast Asia, while HIV infections among young Filipinos have risen over the past decade. Supporters argued that schools needed a more consistent approach to sexuality education.

Comprehensive Sexuality Education, or CSE, was not new. DepEd had already adopted it through Department Order No. 31 in 2018.

The problem, advocates said, was implementation.

Some schools emphasized it. Others barely touched it. SB 1979 sought to institutionalize the program nationwide and ensure that sexuality education was taught consistently across the country.

The legislation established the framework.

The curriculum itself would remain under the authority of the Department of Education.

That distinction became crucial.

As criticism mounted, Hontiveros repeatedly pointed out that claims about masturbation lessons for children could not be found anywhere in the text of the bill.

She was correct.

The bill never mentioned masturbation.

Not once.

Yet by January 2025, the debate was no longer centered on what SB 1979 explicitly said.

The question had become what people believed it would eventually allow.

Three Concerns That Changed the Debate

Critics focused on three features of the proposal.

The first was its scope.

The bill envisioned sexuality education across all levels of basic education, from kindergarten through senior high school. For supporters, that meant age-appropriate learning throughout a child's development. For critics, it meant that a framework intended to address teenage pregnancy and adolescent health would also apply to children as young as four or five years old—a prospect that alarmed many parents.

The second was the phrase “international standards.”

Project Dalisay and other opponents linked that language to sexuality education frameworks used by international organizations and some European countries. Once that connection was established, public discussion quickly shifted from the wording of SB 1979 to examples drawn from foreign sexuality education documents.

The distinction between Philippine policy and international reference materials became increasingly blurred.

The controversy was not unique to the Philippines. Around the world, CSE has become one of the most contested areas of public education. Critics argue that some CSE frameworks introduce sexual concepts too early, conflict with religious or cultural values, produce disputed outcomes, and include gender-related content that many parents believe should be addressed within the family rather than the classroom.

Those criticisms closely mirrored the concerns raised by Project Dalisay. By January 2025, a debate that began with adolescent pregnancy had expanded into a broader argument about culture, values, and who should shape children's understanding of sexuality.

The third concern involved parental authority.

Unlike some education systems that provide parents with formal opt-out mechanisms for sexuality education lessons, SB 1979 contained no parental opt-out provision.

For many parents, that became the most important issue.

The debate ceased to be solely about preventing teenage pregnancy.

It became a debate about who should have the final say over what children learn in school.

Why Singapore Entered the Conversation

As the controversy grew, one country kept appearing in discussions.

Singapore.

Opponents frequently pointed to Singapore's low adolescent pregnancy rate and argued that it had achieved those results without adopting CSE as promoted by international organizations.

The comparison became powerful.

Unlike the Philippine approach proposed under SB 1979, Singapore's sexuality education program is delivered through dedicated sessions rather than broad integration across multiple subjects. Parents are given greater involvement through opt-out mechanisms, and the curriculum emphasizes responsibility, family values, abstinence, and personal decision-making alongside biological education.

For critics, Singapore demonstrated that teenage pregnancy could be addressed without embracing the broader CSE framework.

Supporters countered that Singapore's outcomes reflected many factors beyond sexuality education, including economic conditions, governance, family structures, and social norms.

But the comparison resonated.

If Singapore could achieve better outcomes through a different model, many parents asked, why couldn't the Philippines?

That question would soon move from social media into the halls of government.

The Turning Point

The controversy reached its peak on January 20, 2025.

President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. publicly criticized the bill, calling some of the reported proposals "ridiculous" and "abhorrent." He warned that he would veto the measure unless substantial changes were made.

The pressure intensified immediately.

Two days later, Hontiveros introduced a substitute bill. References to "international standards" were removed. Additional protections for religious freedom were added. The scope of sexuality education provisions was narrowed.

The revisions reflected an acknowledgment of the concerns that had dominated public discussion. But by then, the debate had already moved beyond the bill's specific provisions.

On the same day, six senators withdrew their signatures from the committee report supporting the measure.

Soon afterward, lawmakers began calling for an entirely different approach. Rather than CSE, they argued that schools should focus on reproductive health education centered on adolescent pregnancy prevention, disease awareness, and age-appropriate health instruction.

The debate had changed.

The question was no longer how to amend the bill.

It was whether the bill should survive at all.

Once opponents connected mandatory implementation, international standards, and the absence of parental opt-outs into a single narrative, the debate shifted away from legislative language and toward trust.

What Replaced It

The bill never became law.

But the controversy it generated did not end with its collapse.

In 2025, the Department of Education moved away from CSE and introduced a new Reproductive Health Education framework.

The shift was significant.

Formal reproductive health instruction would focus primarily on learners aged 10 to 19, beginning in Grade 5. Younger children would continue receiving lessons on body awareness, hygiene, safety, and personal development, but CSE as a national framework would be phased out.

The terminology changed.

The structure changed.

The age coverage changed.

In many respects, the new policy reflected the concerns critics had raised throughout the controversy: a greater focus on adolescent reproductive health, less emphasis on comprehensive sexuality education, and a clearer distinction between younger children and older students.

For supporters of the change, the new framework restored a stronger role for parents and narrowed classroom instruction to topics directly related to adolescent health.

For supporters of the original bill, it represented a retreat from a broader approach that they believed was necessary to address teenage pregnancy, HIV, and other reproductive health challenges.

Either way, the debate that began with Senate Bill 1979 had ultimately reshaped national policy.

The Lesson

Hontiveros was correct about the bill's text.

SB 1979 did not mandate masturbation lessons.

But the controversy that consumed the bill was never ultimately about the bill's text.

Once opponents connected mandatory implementation, international standards, and the absence of parental opt-outs into a single narrative, the debate shifted away from legislative language and toward trust.

Trust in institutions.

Trust in curriculum writers.

Trust in future administrators.

And trust in who would ultimately decide what Filipino children should learn.

The Prevention of Adolescent Pregnancy Act was written to address teenage pregnancy.

Yet the national conversation that followed was largely about something else.

Parental authority.

Curriculum control.

And whether parents would have a meaningful voice in decisions involving their children.

The phrase “Not My Job to Write Lesson Plans” was intended as a legal distinction. Hontiveros was arguing that lawmakers write policy while educators write curriculum.

But for many critics, that distinction became the problem rather than the solution.

If the curriculum would be written later, they asked, who would write it? What standards would guide it? And what role would parents have once the framework was already embedded in law?

Those questions proved more powerful than the bill's actual text.

The bill did not collapse because of what was written inside it. It collapsed because many Filipinos became more concerned about who might write the curriculum tomorrow than what the legislation said today.

The Prevention of Adolescent Pregnancy Act never reached a final Senate vote.

But the debate it triggered reshaped Philippine education policy, ended CSE as a national framework, and returned the conversation to a question that remains unresolved:

Who should decide what children learn about sexuality—the state, educators, international organizations, or parents?
